That’s All It Takes to Lose Everything

The SSI asset limit is $2,000. A single well-intentioned gift, a life insurance payout to the wrong name, or the wrong type of trust can disqualify your child from Medicaid, SSI, and waiver services they depend on. One mistake. Lifetime consequences.

Why Special Needs Planning Is Different

Most financial advisors have never encountered the decisions your family faces. Benefit preservation, trust structures, tax-advantaged savings, estate planning, and investment management all have to work as a single integrated system. Every piece affects every other piece.

The families who get this right have an advisor who understands not just the financial mechanics, but the weight of what’s at stake. That’s where FamilyVest is different.

What We Coordinate

Every area works together. We build the plan. Your special needs attorney drafts the legal documents.

Benefit Preservation

SSI, SSDI, Medicaid, and state DD waivers. We structure finances so your dependent keeps eligibility while supplementing quality of life.

Trust Planning

Third-party SNTs for family wealth, first-party SNTs for individual assets, pooled trusts when appropriate. The right vehicle for the right dollars.

ABLE Accounts

Tax-advantaged savings for disability expenses. Up to $100K without affecting SSI. Expanded in 2026 to disability onset before age 46.

Estate Coordination

Wills, trusts, beneficiary designations—structured so no asset accidentally flows directly to your dependent. Backup SNT provisions included.

Letter of Intent

Your child’s routines, preferences, medical needs, and your wishes for their care. The document that guides future caregivers and trustees.

Lifetime Funding

The supplemental care gap can exceed $2M over a lifetime. We build the plan to fund it—through insurance, investments, and family contributions.

Mistakes That Cost Families the Most

These are the errors we see most often. Every one of them is preventable.

1
Naming a disabled beneficiary directly on life insurance, IRAs, or bank accounts. Direct inheritance triggers benefit disqualification. Assets must flow through a properly drafted third-party SNT.
2
Using a first-party trust when a third-party would work. First-party trusts require Medicaid payback at death. Third-party trusts do not. If the money comes from family, it must be a third-party trust.
3
No backup SNT provisions in the family estate plan. If a non-disabled beneficiary later becomes disabled, assets flowing to them from a general trust can disqualify them from benefits.
4
Making trust distributions that count as income. Payments for shelter from an SNT reduce SSI. Distributions should go directly to vendors, never to the beneficiary.
5
Not applying for DD waiver services early enough. Waitlists run years in most states. Your place in line is set by the date of first request, not when you need it.

The families who navigate this well don’t do it alone. They have someone who understands the rules, the stakes, and the weight of getting it right.
